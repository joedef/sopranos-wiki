# Music

<p class="epigraph">Chase personally selected almost every piece of music in 86 episodes.
</p>

There is **no score**. That's the first thing to understand. *The Sopranos* has almost no original underscoring — the music is all source cues, chosen individually, and it does the work a score would.

---

## How it worked

[David Chase](../people/david-chase.md) selected the tracks himself, with music producer **Martin Bruestle** and music editor **Kathryn Dayak**, occasionally consulting **[Steven Van Zandt](../people/steven-van-zandt.md)**.

This is unusual. Most showrunners delegate to a music supervisor. Chase treated song choice as writing, and the budget — HBO money, no advertiser sensitivities, and a show important enough to clear expensive rights — let him get almost anything he wanted.

He was a musician before he was a writer, and it shows.

---

## The closing song

The show's signature device: **a different song over the end credits of nearly every episode**, chosen as a comment on what just happened.

Sometimes it's straightforward. Often it's ironic. Occasionally it's a joke that only lands a season later. The habit trained a generation of viewers to sit through credits, and it's the single most imitated thing about the show's music.

A few of the best:

- **"Thru and Thru"** (The Rolling Stones) over the end of **"Funhouse"** — [Big Pussy](../characters/big-pussy.md) dead, Meadow's graduation party, the crew going about their business. Chase has named it as a favorite.
- **"Living on a Thin Line"** (The Kinks) in "University" — after Tracee's murder, over the Bing.
- **"Black Books"** (Nils Lofgren) at the end of "Long Term Parking."
- **"Comfortably Numb"** (the Van Morrison/Roger Waters version) closing "The Test Dream."
- **"Space Monkey"** (Placebo), **"Glad Tidings"** (Van Morrison), and a great deal of Bob Dylan, Elvis Costello, and Otis Redding.

## "Woke Up This Morning"

The main title: **Alabama 3's "Woke Up This Morning (Chosen One Mix)"** — a British acid-house-blues group, which is a strange provenance for the most recognizable television theme of its era.

The song predates the show and was written about a real case: a woman who killed her abusive partner. Chase heard it and used it essentially unchanged.

**The title sequence** is Tony driving from the Lincoln Tunnel through Newark and Jersey City and out along the Turnpike to the suburbs — the whole show in ninety seconds, a man leaving the city his father worked in for a house that money bought.

**The World Trade Center** appears in his side mirror for the first three seasons and was removed after September 2001. Some streaming versions carry the original; some don't.

## "Don't Stop Believin'"

Tony selects it from a tabletop jukebox in the final scene. It is the last thing anyone hears before the cut to black, and it stops mid-word.

**It is not the joke people assume.** The common reading — that Chase chose a naff arena-rock anthem ironically — doesn't survive contact with his own account: he has said the ceiling he was going for was *don't stop believing*, simple and on the nose.

Two other things worth knowing:

- **Journey's only condition** for licensing was that the scene contain no violence and no deaths. Which, depending on your reading of the ending, they either got or very cleverly didn't.
- Chase reportedly considered a number of alternatives, and the choice was made late.

The song's own subsequent life — karaoke, sports arenas, *Glee* — has made it harder to hear as Chase intended, which is an accident of history rather than a flaw in the choice.

## The Italian music

A separate and deliberate strand.

**[Dominic Chianese](../people/dominic-chianese.md)** is a genuine singer, and his performances on the show are real — most memorably the Neapolitan song **"Core 'ngrato"** at a wake, which stops the episode dead.

Italian and Italian-American music recurs at funerals, weddings, and the restaurant scenes, and it's always doing the same job: marking the gap between the culture these men claim and the New Jersey suburb they actually live in. See [Italian-American Identity](italian-american-identity.md).

## The influence

Before *The Sopranos*, television drama scored itself. After it, **needle drops became the default grammar** of prestige television — *Mad Men*, *Breaking Bad*, *The Leftovers*, *Atlanta*, and essentially every HBO drama since.

The specific innovation wasn't using pop songs. It was treating the closing song as **the last line of the episode**.
