# Mob Movies & Self-Awareness

<p class="epigraph">Just when I thought I was out… they pull me back in.
<cite>Silvio Dante, doing Pacino, on request, badly, for years</cite></p>

The show's defining formal move: **its characters have seen the films it's descended from, and are consciously imitating them.**

---

## The premise

Every previous mob fiction assumed its characters didn't know they were in one. *The Sopranos* assumes the opposite, and builds everything on it.

These men have all seen ***The Godfather***. They quote it, argue about it, rank the installments, and measure themselves against it. [Tony](../characters/tony-soprano.md) prefers *Part II*. [Silvio](../characters/silvio-dante.md) does the *Part III* line on request. [Paulie](../characters/paulie-walnuts.md) and [Big Pussy](../characters/big-pussy.md) debate the films' details as though they were scripture.

They have also seen ***Goodfellas***, and behave accordingly.

**The consequence is corrosive.** These are men whose sense of what a gangster is comes from fiction about gangsters, which was itself romanticized. They are performing a copy of a copy, and falling short of it, and know they're falling short.

Tony's opening line — that he came in at the end and the best is over — is partly a statement about the business and partly a statement about **the movies**. He's comparing his life to *The Godfather* and finding it wanting.

## The casting argument

**Twenty-seven actors** appear in both *Goodfellas* and *The Sopranos*, including three regulars.

This isn't budget. It's a claim.

| Actor | *Goodfellas* | *The Sopranos* |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **[Lorraine Bracco](../people/lorraine-bracco.md)** | Karen Hill, the mob wife | **[Dr. Melfi](../characters/dr-melfi.md)**, the psychiatrist |
| **[Michael Imperioli](../people/michael-imperioli.md)** | Spider — shot in the foot | **[Christopher](../characters/christopher-moltisanti.md)** — shoots a man in the foot |
| **Frank Vincent** | Billy Batts | **[Phil Leotardo](../characters/phil-leotardo.md)** |
| **[Tony Sirico](../people/tony-sirico.md)** | Tony Stacks | **[Paulie](../characters/paulie-walnuts.md)** |
| **Suzanne Shepherd** | Karen's mother | Carmela's mother |

Bracco's is the pointed one: she asked for Melfi *because* she'd played the wife, and the show gets to have the woman from *Goodfellas* sit across from the gangster and diagnose him.

Imperioli's is the joke: Spider gets shot in the foot by Joe Pesci for being slow; Christopher shoots a bakery clerk in the foot for being slow. Imperioli has confirmed it's deliberate.

**[Dominic Chianese](../people/dominic-chianese.md)** was Johnny Ola in *The Godfather Part II*. Casting him as [Junior](../characters/junior-soprano.md) does the same work in the other direction.

## Cleaver

The most complete statement of the theme.

[Christopher](../characters/christopher-moltisanti.md), an aspiring screenwriter who has read a book about story structure and wants an *arc*, eventually makes ***Cleaver*** — a slasher film in which a mob boss is murdered by his protégé, who then rises.

Tony watches it and understands **exactly** what it is: a confession, a threat, and a wish, dressed as genre.

And he can say nothing, because it's a movie. The show has arrived at the position where its characters use fiction to say things they can't say directly — which is what *The Sopranos* itself is doing about the genre it belongs to.

## "D-Girl" and Hollywood

**"D-Girl"** (S2E20) sends Christopher to meet a development executive and discover that people like him are **content** — raw material for other people's careers.

It's the show's first sustained joke at the expense of its own industry, and it's uncomfortable on purpose: the audience watching this is doing exactly what the D-girl does.

## The argument with Goodfellas

*The Sopranos* is often called *Goodfellas* on television, and Scorsese's film is unquestionably its formal parent — the voiceover-free naturalism, the comedy, the sudden violence, the cast.

But the show is also **arguing with it**.

*Goodfellas* is about the seduction and the crash: it makes the life look wonderful for two hours so the ending lands. *The Sopranos* never makes it look wonderful. Nobody in it is having a good time. The money is spent on bad furniture. The restaurants are mediocre. The men are unhappy, medicated, and bored.

That's the correction: the genre had spent seventy years insisting these lives were *operatic*, and Chase's answer is that they're **suburban**. See [America in Decline](america-in-decline.md).

---

## The audience in the trap

The last turn of the screw.

The show's characters romanticize gangsters via movies. The show's *audience* is romanticizing gangsters via a television series. The structure is identical, and the series knows it — which is why it keeps making Tony charming and then making you watch what he does.

Chase's contempt for viewers who wanted "justice" at the end comes from here: the demand for a satisfying punishment is the demand for the genre's traditional absolution, and he refused to supply it. See [Reading the Ending](reading-the-ending.md).
