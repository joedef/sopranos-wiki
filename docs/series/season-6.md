## Part one: the coma

[Junior](../characters/junior-soprano.md), whose dementia has advanced past the point of recognition, shoots Tony in the stomach in "Members Only." Tony spends the next two episodes in a coma.

**"Join the Club" and "Mayham"** are the show's most radical hours. In them, Tony is a mild-mannered optics salesman at a conference in Costa Mesa who has lost his wallet and been mistaken for a man named **Kevin Finnerty**. He can't get home. His voice isn't Gandolfini's usual register. A house on a hill is holding a party he's expected at, and a group of monks are furious with Finnerty over a faulty heating system.

Chase refuses to explain it, and it doesn't need explaining: it's a man being offered a life that isn't his, and a destination he isn't ready for. *Infinity. Finnerty.*

He wakes up grateful. "Every day is a gift," he tells people. It lasts about four episodes.

**The other major thread is [Vito Spatafore](../characters/vito-spatafore.md)** — a top earner outed as gay, who flees to New Hampshire, finds a genuinely happy life with a short-order cook, and comes back anyway because he can't imagine being anyone else. What happens to him is the show's bluntest statement about the culture it depicts.

## Part two: the collapse

Nine episodes of dismantling.

**"Soprano Home Movies"** — Tony and [Bobby](../characters/bobby-baccalieri.md) at a lake house; a drunken Monopoly game turns into a real fistfight, and Tony, having lost, sends Bobby to commit his first murder. Contains Bobby's line about how you probably don't hear it when it happens — which Chase cuts back to in "The Blue Comet."

**"Kennedy and Heidi"** — [Christopher](../characters/christopher-moltisanti.md), high, crashes his car with Tony in the passenger seat. A baby seat is crushed. Tony leans in and suffocates him. Then he flies to Las Vegas, takes peyote with a woman Christopher knew, wins at roulette, and shouts *I get it!* at the desert sunrise. What he gets is that he is relieved his nephew is dead.

**"The Second Coming"** — A.J.'s suicide attempt in the pool, and Tony pulling him out. Gandolfini's rawest scene in the series.

**"The Blue Comet"** — [Bobby](../characters/bobby-baccalieri.md) is killed in a model train shop; [Silvio](../characters/silvio-dante.md) is left in a coma; Melfi, having read a study suggesting talk therapy helps sociopaths refine their manipulation, **ends the therapy** after seven years. The crew goes to the mattresses.

**"Made in America"** — the finale. See **[The Ending](../the-ending.md)**.

## Reception

Metacritic **96**. The season won the **Primetime Emmy for Outstanding Drama Series** in 2007, and Chase won Outstanding Writing for the finale.

The finale's reception was violently mixed on the night and has settled into near-consensus admiration. That story is on its own page: **[The Ending](../the-ending.md)** for the facts, **[Reading the Ending](../analysis/reading-the-ending.md)** for the arguments.

---

## What the season is arguing

Part one gives Tony the closest thing to grace the show allows: a near-death experience, an alternate self, and a genuine conviction that he's been given another chance.

Part two is the answer. He murders his nephew and feels relief. He gambles compulsively. He loses his therapist — who leaves not because he's incurable but because she concludes she has been **helping him get better at being what he is**. And by the last episode, everyone who kept him human is dead, comatose, estranged, or has walked away.

The cut to black arrives after six seasons of a man being offered chances to change and declining every one. Whether that's a tragedy or a diagnosis is the thing people are still arguing about.
