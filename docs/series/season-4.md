## The season about money

The standard complaint is that nothing happens. That's close to true and entirely deliberate.

The economy has tightened. The earners aren't earning. Tony spends the season on schemes that are small, grubby, and legally exposed — an HUD housing fraud, a phone card scam, an esplanade contract. [Paulie](../characters/paulie-walnuts.md) is in jail in Youngstown and sulking about who's paying for it. [Christopher](../characters/christopher-moltisanti.md) is on heroin. [Junior's](../characters/junior-soprano.md) RICO trial grinds on and his mind is going.

And Carmela, who has spent four seasons declining to ask where the money comes from, becomes obsessed with the fact that **none of it is in her name**. Her scenes with the financial adviser, and later with Tony about a life insurance policy, are the season's spine. She isn't having a moral crisis. She's doing due diligence.

Meanwhile [Furio](../characters/furio-giunta.md) is in love with her, and she with him, and neither of them does anything about it — which is somehow worse than if they had.

## "Whoever Did This"

The season's turn, and one of the show's great hours.

[Ralph Cifaretto's](../characters/ralph-cifaretto.md) son is critically injured in an archery accident. Ralph, genuinely destroyed, becomes briefly sympathetic — Pantoliano plays it without a shred of the character's usual performance. Then the racehorse Pie-O-My, which Tony had come to love more than most people, dies in a stable fire that Ralph probably set for the insurance.

Tony confronts him in his kitchen. The fight is ugly, clumsy, and not choreographed like television — two middle-aged men in a small room. Tony kills him with his hands.

Then he and Christopher spend the rest of the episode dismembering and disposing of the body, arguing about logistics, and Tony gets a call from his son.

**Why it matters:** Tony kills Ralph over a horse, not over Tracee, and the show knows exactly what that says. He also violates the code by killing a made man without sanction, and spends the rest of the series quietly managing that lie.

## "Whitecaps"

The finale is 75 minutes long and contains no crime.

Tony puts a deposit on a shore house. Then a former mistress calls the house and tells Carmela everything. What follows is an extended, escalating fight across a whole episode — the accumulated grievances of a twenty-year marriage delivered at full volume in a kitchen, a bedroom, and a hallway.

Carmela says the thing she can't take back: she names [Furio](../characters/furio-giunta.md), tells Tony she doesn't love him, and puts him out of the house.

**Gandolfini and Falco both won Emmys** off this episode, and it's obvious why. There's no gun in it and it's the most frightening hour in the series — because for the first time Tony encounters something he genuinely cannot fix by force, favor, or charm.

## Also notable

**"Christopher" (S4E42)** — Columbus Day, and a whole episode of Italian-American characters arguing about Italian-American representation. Written by [Michael Imperioli](../people/michael-imperioli.md). Widely disliked on broadcast, and the most direct thing the show ever said about its own critics. See [Italian-American Identity](../analysis/italian-american-identity.md).

**"Everybody Hurts" (S4E45)** — Artie's suicide attempt and Tony's realization that his generosity is a form of control.

**"Eloise" (S4E51)** — Carmela and Meadow at the Plaza, one of the show's best two-hander scenes about class and self-deception.

**"Calling All Cars" (S4E50)** — Tony walks out of therapy, and the season's dream sequences turn genuinely opaque.

## Reception

The ratings peak of the entire run: a **13.43M** premiere and a **10.99M** season average. Critically strong at the time and rising since — the "nothing happens" complaint has aged into the observation that a show about a business in a downturn is *supposed* to feel like that.

Three acting Emmys in 2003: **Gandolfini**, **Falco**, and **[Joe Pantoliano](../people/joe-pantoliano.md)**, whose win came for a character killed off in episode nine.
