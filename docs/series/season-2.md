## The season

**Three threads, braided.**

**[Big Pussy](../characters/big-pussy.md) is wearing a wire.** He disappeared at the end of season one; he comes back in episode two with a story about his back. The audience learns the truth in "From Where to Eternity"; Tony doesn't confirm it until the finale. The whole season is watching a man have dinner with someone he's going to have to kill.

**[Richie Aprile](../characters/richie-aprile.md) comes home.** Jackie's brother is out after ten years, and he cannot accept that the business changed while he was inside. He is a genuinely frightening piece of writing — a man whose sense of grievance is total, whose leather jacket is a legal document, and who gets involved with [Janice](../characters/janice-soprano.md), which turns out to be the most dangerous thing he does.

**Tony's world professionalizes.** He brings [Furio](../characters/furio-giunta.md) back from Naples. **Bust Out** — the systematic looting of Davey Scatino's sporting goods store through a credit line until it's a husk — is the clearest, coldest procedural explanation of how the business actually works.

## "Funhouse"

The finale, and one of the show's formal high points.

Tony gets food poisoning from bad Indian takeout and spends most of the episode in a fever, dreaming. In the dreams he already knows what he has spent thirteen episodes refusing to know, and a talking fish on a boardwalk — in [Big Pussy's](../characters/big-pussy.md) voice — tells him so.

He wakes up, and he and Silvio and Paulie take Pussy out on the boat.

The scene that follows is a good candidate for the show's best. Pussy knows. Tony knows. Everyone is polite about it. He asks not to be shot in the face. Afterward the men are quiet on the ride back, and Tony throws up over the side.

Then the episode cuts to **Meadow's high school graduation party**, and the last sequence intercuts the family celebrating with the crew going about their business, scored to The Rolling Stones' "Thru and Thru" — an ending Chase has cited as one of his favorites in the series.

## Also notable

**"D-Girl" (S2E20)** — Christopher goes to Hollywood, meets a development executive, and finds out he's a story someone else wants to option. The show's first sustained joke at the expense of its own genre.

**"Commendatori" (S2E17)** — Tony, Paulie, and Christopher go to Naples. Tony discovers the old country doesn't operate the way the mythology said, and that the actual boss over there is a woman.

**"The Happy Wanderer" (S2E19)** — the Executive Game. Davey Scatino gambles himself into ruin at a card table while Tony watches, and the episode's real subject is Tony's contempt for civilians who want to be near the life.

## Reception

Metacritic **97**; Rotten Tomatoes 94%. **[James Gandolfini](../people/james-gandolfini.md) won his first Emmy** for Lead Actor. **Golden Globes** went to Gandolfini, Falco, and **[Nancy Marchand](../people/nancy-marchand.md)** for Supporting Actress — Marchand's only major win for Livia, and her last. She died on **June 18, 2000**, two months after the finale aired.

The show also received its first **Peabody Award** for this stretch.
