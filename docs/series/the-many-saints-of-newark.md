## The film

The protagonist is **Dickie Moltisanti** — [Christopher's](../characters/christopher-moltisanti.md) father, referenced constantly in the series as a saint and a legend, and here revealed as neither. He is charming, violent, self-deceiving, and quite a lot like the man his nephew grows up to be.

Around him: the **1967 Newark riots** and the collapse of the city; the fracturing partnership between the Italian mob and Harold McBrayer, a Black associate who builds his own operation; Dickie's father Hollywood Dick and his young Italian bride; and, at the edges, a teenage Tony who idolizes his uncle and is slowly being handed a worldview.

Christopher narrates — from the family plot, an inspired and slightly cruel choice given what Tony eventually does to him.

The framing thesis is that Tony's formation isn't one thing but a series of small withdrawals: a father in prison, a mother who cannot give affection, and the one adult who actually pays attention to him being a murderer.

---

## What works

**Alessandro Nivola** carries it. Dickie is a genuinely good creation: a man performing generosity while being incapable of it.

**Vera Farmiga's Livia** is the film's best piece of connective tissue — not an impression of [Nancy Marchand](../people/nancy-marchand.md), but a recognizable earlier draft of the same person, and the film's funniest and bleakest scenes are hers.

**Michael Gandolfini** does something genuinely difficult with enormous restraint. He doesn't do his father's voice. What he does is the posture and the watchfulness, and there are two or three moments — a look across a room, the way he takes a seat — that land hard for anyone who has seen the series.

**Ray Liotta** plays twins and is excellent as both. It was one of his last performances; he died in May 2022.

---

## What doesn't

**It's structurally overloaded.** The film is simultaneously a Dickie Moltisanti biography, a Newark riots drama, a Harold McBrayer story, and a Tony Soprano origin — and it has two hours. The McBrayer material in particular is the most interesting thread and the most starved.

**Tony is a supporting character in his own prequel**, which is defensible and also the reason a lot of the audience felt short-changed.

**The explanation problem.** The series' great strength was that Tony was never fully explicable. The film's job is to explain him. Even done well, that's a subtraction.

**Manohla Dargis** in *The New York Times* called it an unnecessary and disappointingly ordinary origin story. **Richard Roeper** called it a sharply honed, darkly funny companion piece. Both are defensible readings of the same film.

## Commercial performance

It went out theatrically and on **HBO Max simultaneously**, in October 2021, in a market still deranged by the pandemic.

Theatrically it failed: **$13.1M worldwide on a ~$50M budget**, with a **C+ CinemaScore** — an unusually poor exit score, which generally reflects an audience that wanted something other than what it got.

On streaming it did well: over **1 million streams in the opening weekend** and **2.1 million households within a month**, and it produced a measurable spike in viewership of the original series, which is arguably what it was really for.

## A sequel?

Chase has said he'd be interested in a follow-up covering Tony in his twenties, and **Terence Winter** has been publicly enthusiastic. Chase has also said the studio hasn't prioritized it.

Nothing has been produced. See [After The Sopranos](after-the-sopranos.md).

---

## Is it canon?

Yes, in the sense that Chase co-wrote it and it doesn't contradict the series in any load-bearing way. It does fill in things the show deliberately left vague — how Dickie died, what Tony's childhood actually looked like, why Junior is the way he is — and whether that's a gift or a loss is a matter of taste.

The series works without it. It does not work without the series.
