## The season

**The premise arrives fully formed.** Tony Soprano collapses at a family barbecue and starts seeing a psychiatrist. He is a capo in the DiMeo family, acting under his uncle [Junior](../characters/junior-soprano.md) while boss Jackie Aprile is dying of cancer. His mother [Livia](../characters/livia-soprano.md) is being moved into a retirement community against her will and has decided to make everyone pay for it.

**Two wars run in parallel.** The external one is over succession: Junior gets the title, Tony keeps the earners, and the arrangement holds until it doesn't. The internal one is with Livia, who cannot be beaten because she never says anything actionable. She feeds Junior's paranoia in a series of conversations that are, individually, entirely deniable.

**The hit.** Junior orders Tony killed. Tony survives. And in **"I Dream of Jeannie Cusamano,"** Melfi's questions finally force him to say out loud what he has spent thirteen episodes refusing to think: his mother helped arrange it. He goes to the nursing home to kill her, and she's being wheeled out with what looks like a stroke, smiling.

## "College"

The fifth episode is the one that made the show, and it nearly didn't air as written.

Tony drives [Meadow](../characters/meadow-soprano.md) to look at colleges in Maine. In the car she asks him directly whether he's in the Mafia — the first time anyone in his family says it aloud — and he gives her a partial, careful truth. It's the warmest father-daughter material in the series.

He also spots **Fabian "Febby" Petrulio**, a former associate who turned witness and is living under an assumed name. Between campus visits, Tony finds him and garrotes him.

**HBO executives objected.** Their argument was straightforward: a protagonist who commits a cold-blooded, premeditated murder in episode five will lose the audience. Chase's counter-argument was the opposite — that the audience would lose *respect* for Tony if he let a rat walk, and that the show's honesty depended on it.

Chase won. The episode won the **Primetime Emmy for Outstanding Writing**, *TV Guide* later ranked it **#2 on its 100 Greatest Episodes of All Time**, and both *Time* and *Entertainment Weekly* have called it the best episode of the series.

The whole antihero era of American television — *The Shield*, *Breaking Bad*, *Mad Men*, *Dexter* — descends from that argument being won.

Meanwhile, back in New Jersey, Carmela and **Father Phil** share wine, communion, and a near-miss that she will be confessing to for years.

## Also notable

**"Down Neck" (S1E7)** — the first extended flashback, establishing Johnny Boy, young Livia, and where Tony's panic attacks come from. The template for the show's use of memory.

**"Isabella" (S1E12)** — Tony, heavily medicated, hallucinates an Italian woman nursing a baby next door. One of the show's clearest statements about what he's actually missing, and the episode immediately preceding the hit.

**"Nobody Knows Anything" (S1E11)** — [Big Pussy](../characters/big-pussy.md) is suspected of wearing a wire. Planted here, paid off a whole season later.

---

## Reception

Immediate and near-universal acclaim. It won the **Golden Globe for Best Drama Series**, the writing Emmy for "College," and **[Edie Falco's](../people/edie-falco.md) first Emmy** for Lead Actress.

The audience was still small — a 3.46M average, tiny against what the show became — because HBO in 1999 was a fraction of the size it would be by 2002. What the season produced instead was the thing that mattered more: the critical consensus that television could do this.
