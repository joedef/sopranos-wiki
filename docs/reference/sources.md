# Sources & Further Reading

What this wiki was built from, roughly in order of how much weight it should carry.

---

## Primary

**The episodes themselves** — all 86, plus [*The Many Saints of Newark*](../series/the-many-saints-of-newark.md) (2021).

***Wise Guy: David Chase and The Sopranos*** **(2024)** — Alex Gibney's two-part HBO documentary. The most substantial on-the-record account [Chase](../people/david-chase.md) has given, and the closest thing to an authorized history.

***Talking Sopranos*** **(2020–22)** — [Michael Imperioli](../people/michael-imperioli.md) and Steve Schirripa's episode-by-episode rewatch podcast, with guests including most of the surviving cast and Chase himself. **More first-hand production detail than any book**, and the single most useful source for anyone going deep. Caveat: it's two friendly participants reminiscing, not journalism.

**DVD and Blu-ray commentaries and supplements** — several episodes carry commentary from Chase, directors, and cast.

**Contemporaneous interviews** — particularly anything before about 2010, which predates the worst of the retrospective smoothing.

---

## Documented secondary

**Alan Sepinwall & Matt Zoller Seitz, *The Sopranos Sessions*** (2019). **The essential book.** Episode-by-episode critical analysis by the two best television critics of the era, plus long interviews with Chase — including the exchange in which he refers to "that death scene" and then says *"Fuck you guys."* If you buy one thing, buy this.

**Brett Martin, *Difficult Men*** (2013). The definitive account of the showrunner era — *The Sopranos*, *The Wire*, *Deadwood*, *Mad Men*, *Breaking Bad* — and very good on Chase's working method and temperament.

**Alan Sepinwall, *The Revolution Was Televised*** (2012). The broader argument about what changed and why.

**Peter Biskind, *Down and Dirty Pictures*** and his *Vanity Fair* reporting on HBO — context on the network's rise. Biskind is enormously readable and should be treated with some caution; his sourcing is anecdotal and subjects have disputed him.

**Glen O. Gabbard, *The Psychology of The Sopranos*** (2002). A practicing psychoanalyst on the therapy material. Uneven, and genuinely useful on whether [Melfi's](../characters/dr-melfi.md) practice is plausible.

**Regina Barreca (ed.), *A Sitdown with the Sopranos*** (2002). Academic essays, mostly on Italian-American identity, from the middle of the run.

**Contemporaneous criticism** — Sepinwall's *Star-Ledger* episode reviews (written from the show's own home paper, which the show occasionally references), Emily Nussbaum in *The New Yorker*, and Matt Zoller Seitz.

## On the real history

**Selwyn Raab, *Five Families*** (2005) — the standard history of the New York organization, with substantial material on New Jersey.

**FBI and Department of Justice records** on the [DeCavalcante family](real-world-mafia.md), including the surveillance in which real members discuss the show.

**Anthony Bruno's reporting** and contemporaneous *Star-Ledger* and *New York Times* coverage of the DeCavalcante prosecutions, 1999–2005.

## On reliability

Three standing cautions.

**Chase's statements about the ending are a moving target.** He has said materially different things in 2007, 2015, 2019, 2021, and 2024. Any source quoting one of them as definitive is misleading by omission. [The Ending](../the-ending.md) presents them all with dates.

**"Chase reveals the ending" coverage is usually wrong.** A November 2021 *Hollywood Reporter* piece was very widely misreported as a confirmation that Tony dies. Chase's own response was that if people believed it, it worked for him — and he clarified again in 2024. Be extremely skeptical of any headline in this genre.

**Cast anecdotes have been polished.** Twenty-five years of conventions, podcasts, and anniversary interviews reward a good story. Where accounts conflict, this wiki says who claims what rather than picking one.
