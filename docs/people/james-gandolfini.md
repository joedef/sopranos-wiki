## Before

A Jersey kid — Park Ridge, a bricklayer-and-custodian father from Parma, Rutgers, then a decade of bar work in Manhattan while acting on the side. He didn't start seriously until his mid-twenties, after a friend took him to an acting class.

His breakthrough was **Virgil in *True Romance*** (1993) — a small part in which he beats Patricia Arquette in a motel room and is very frightening and very still. That's the tape that got him the *Sopranos* audition.

Also: *Get Shorty*, *Crimson Tide*, *The Juror*, *A Civil Action*. He was a working character actor with a specialty in heavies, and he was 37.

---

## Casting

He was not the obvious choice and knew it. He has said he assumed they'd want a movie star, and that he read for it expecting nothing.

**[Steven Van Zandt](steven-van-zandt.md) had auditioned for Tony** and HBO wanted a trained actor. Chase wrote him [Silvio](../characters/silvio-dante.md) instead.

Gandolfini reportedly stopped his own audition partway through, unhappy with it, and asked to come back. Chase's account is that what he saw was a man who was plausibly both frightening and wounded — and that the second quality was the harder one to find.

---

## The performance

What he does is physical before it's anything else. Tony fills doorways. He takes up a whole booth. He moves like a man managing a body that has become a problem, and the show uses that: the labored breathing on stairs, the hand that goes to the chest, the way he lowers himself into chairs.

The tells are tiny and consistent. The mouth working before a lie. The half-second delay before deciding whether to be offended. The laugh that's genuine, and the one that isn't, played differently.

And he's **funny** — which gets underrated in retrospectives, and is a large part of why an audience followed a murderer for six seasons.

**His methods were rough.** He'd stay up all night, drink too much coffee, put a stone in his shoe, and — by several accounts including his own — hit himself, to get to the places the darker episodes needed. He would sometimes disappear for a day or two during production. He apologized for it, repeatedly, and reportedly once gave every member of the crew a substantial cash gift by way of making up for the disruption.

He found the role genuinely difficult to carry and said so. He was, by universal account, nothing like Tony: shy, generous, uncomfortable with fame, and mortified by attention.

---

## The money

He renegotiated with HBO more than once, most dramatically in 2003, in a dispute that briefly stopped production on season five. The show came back with him on a substantially improved deal.

The contemporaneous framing was diva behavior. The retrospective view is that he was the entire commercial engine of a network that had been transformed by his work, and the leverage was real.

---

## After

He deliberately avoided anything Tony-shaped: *In the Loop*, *Where the Wild Things Are* (voice), *Zero Dark Thirty*, *Killing Them Softly*, and Chase's own *Not Fade Away*.

His last lead was ***Enough Said*** (2013), a gentle romantic comedy opposite Julia Louis-Dreyfus, released after his death — a performance of such warmth that it functions, accidentally, as a corrective to eight years of Tony.

He produced two HBO documentaries on returning veterans, *Alive Day Memories* and *Wartorn*, and visited troops repeatedly without publicity.

---

## Death

He died of a **heart attack in Rome on June 19, 2013**, aged 51, while on holiday with his son.

The response was disproportionate for a television actor and entirely proportionate to what he'd meant. Flags in New Jersey. A funeral at the Cathedral of St. John the Divine in Manhattan attended by thousands. Tributes from every showrunner working.

**Michael Gandolfini**, his son, played the teenage Tony in [*The Many Saints of Newark*](../series/the-many-saints-of-newark.md) (2021) — a decision that could have been ghoulish and wasn't, largely because of how carefully he declined to imitate his father.
