# The Writers' Room

Small, stable, and unusually consequential — three of the people in it went on to create defining series of the following decade.

---

## How it worked

**[David Chase](david-chase.md)** ran it as an autocracy, by universal account. He wrote or co-wrote a handful of episodes per season and **rewrote nearly everything else**, sometimes heavily.

Stories were broken in the room and then broken down further — **shot by shot**, during script development — which is a film-production habit rather than a television one and is a large part of why the show looks the way it does. See [Direction & Craft](../analysis/direction-and-craft.md).

**Twenty writers or writing teams** received credit across 86 episodes, but the core was about six people.

---

## The core

### Robin Green & Mitchell Burgess

A married writing team who came with Chase from ***Northern Exposure***. They were with the show from season one and became executive producers.

Their credits are among the best in the run: **"College"**'s companion pieces, **"Employee of the Month"** (Melfi's rape), **"Whoever Did This"** (Ralph's death), **"Cold Cuts,"** and co-writing **"Whitecaps."**

They tend to get the episodes about **women and about damage**, and they're consistently the show's least sentimental writers.

### Terence Winter

Joined in **season two** and became the show's other defining voice — executive producer from season five.

His credits are the ones people quote: **"Pine Barrens," "Long Term Parking," "Members Only," "The Second Coming,"** and a long list besides. Winter writes the funniest dialogue in the series and also its most brutal single hour, frequently in the same episode.

He went on to create ***Boardwalk Empire*** (2010–14) for HBO, starring **[Steve Buscemi](../characters/tony-blundetto.md)**, and co-wrote *The Wolf of Wall Street*.

### Matthew Weiner

Hired for **season five** after Chase read his spec pilot for a period drama about advertising executives. That script was ***Mad Men***, which Weiner made after *The Sopranos* ended.

His *Sopranos* credits include **"Kennedy and Heidi," "The Test Dream"** (with Chase), **"The Blue Comet"** (with Chase), and **"Mayham."** He gravitates toward the dream material and the psychological interiors — which is recognizably the same writer who made *Mad Men*.

### Frank Renzulli

Season one through three, and the writer most responsible for the **texture of the criminal world** — the earning, the slights, the day-to-day mechanics. "Pax Soprana," "The Happy Wanderer," "Amour Fou," "Nobody Knows Anything."

He and Chase reportedly clashed, and he left after season three.

### Others

**Todd A. Kessler** (co-wrote "Funhouse"; later created *Damages*). **Diane Frolov & Andrew Schneider** (season six, including the Vito New Hampshire episodes). **Lawrence Konner** (later co-wrote [*The Many Saints of Newark*](../series/the-many-saints-of-newark.md)). **Jason Cahill**. **James Manos Jr.** (co-wrote "College"; later developed *Dexter*). **Salvatore J. Stabile**. **Michael Caleo**. **Nick Santora**. **Toni Kalem**. **David Flebotte**.

And **[Michael Imperioli](michael-imperioli.md)** — an actor who wrote **five episodes**, which is genuinely rare.

## The alumni effect

The room produced an unusual number of showrunners:

| Writer | Went on to |
| --- | --- |
| **Matthew Weiner** | *Mad Men* |
| **Terence Winter** | *Boardwalk Empire*, *The Wolf of Wall Street* |
| **Todd A. Kessler** | *Damages*, *Bloodline* |
| **James Manos Jr.** | *Dexter* (developed) |

Together with the directors it trained, this is a substantial share of the following fifteen years of prestige television, and it's the most concrete form the show's influence takes. See [Legacy & Influence](../reference/legacy-and-influence.md).
