## Before

A SUNY Purchase graduate — the same programme that produced Stanley Tucci and Ving Rhames — who spent the early 1990s in New York independent film, working repeatedly with **Hal Hartley** and **Nick Gomez** on almost no money.

Television followed: *Law & Order*, *Homicide*, and then a recurring role as corrections officer Diane Whittlesey on HBO's ***Oz*** (1997–2000), which is where the *Sopranos* producers found her.

Tom Fontana's description of her is the useful one: an actress unadorned by any embroidery, who does everything with a simplicity and honesty that is breathtaking.

---

## Carmela

She was cast off *Oz*, and the role is built on a very specific skill: **playing self-deception as effortful**.

Carmela is not stupid and is not lied to. She knows exactly what her husband does. What Falco does — in a jaw, a pause, a half-second of decision before she changes the subject — is show you the *work* of not-knowing happening in real time. It's what makes the character sympathetic when the writing alone would not be.

Her best hour is **"Whitecaps"** (S4E52), the marriage-ending fight, which won both her and [Gandolfini](james-gandolfini.md) Emmys.

Three Emmys for Lead Actress (1999, 2001, 2003) and two Golden Globes.

She has said she **never watched the show while it aired**, and has been notably unsentimental about the character since — declining, in interviews, to defend Carmela's choices.

---

## After

***Nurse Jackie*** (2009–15) — a pill-addicted emergency room nurse, and a fourth Emmy, this time in **comedy**, for a performance about as far from Carmela as the same actress could get.

Also *30 Rock* (nominated), *Law & Order True Crime: The Menendez Murders* (nominated), *Avatar: The Way of Water*, and a Tony nomination for *The House of Blue Leaves* (2011).

She was diagnosed with breast cancer in 2003 — during production on season five — and continued working through treatment without disclosing it until afterward.
