Tony's opposite number, closest peer, and the only person in the series who operates at his level.

---

## The relationship

Johnny Sack lives in New Jersey while running New York business — a geographical oddity that makes him the permanent interface between the two families, and gives him and [Tony](tony-soprano.md) a genuine, wary, sometimes warm working friendship.

They like each other. They understand each other's problems in a way nobody else does. And they are structurally obliged to be adversaries, which they both know and periodically regret.

Their scenes are the show's best depiction of the business as a **business** — two executives from competing firms who golf together, complain about their boards, and would each have the other killed if the numbers required it.

---

## The Ginny joke

The best plotline in season four, and the purest demonstration of how this world works.

[Ralph Cifaretto](ralph-cifaretto.md) makes a crude joke about **Ginny Sacrimoni's** weight. Johnny hears about it and wants Ralph dead. Ralph is a huge earner. Tony refuses to sanction it. Johnny plans to do it anyway. Tony plans a preemptive hit on Johnny.

The two families come within a phone call of open war **over a joke about a woman's weight** — and Chase plays it entirely straight, because in a culture built on respect as currency, an insult *is* a financial event.

It's resolved by Carmine Sr., anticlimactically, and both men have to live with having planned each other's deaths.

---

## The fall

Johnny becomes boss after Carmine Sr.'s death and a succession fight with Little Carmine. He is arrested almost immediately.

His humiliation is drawn out with real cruelty. In **"Mr. & Mrs. John Sacrimoni Request…"** (S6E70), he is granted a furlough to attend his daughter's wedding — and, at the end of the day, is taken back into custody on the lawn, in front of everyone, weeping openly.

The crew's reaction is the show at its most precise: they're not moved. They're **disgusted**. A man cried in public. Tony, who has spent seven years in therapy, is among the most contemptuous — and then goes and reassures himself in a mirror.

Johnny takes a plea — accepting a RICO conviction and admitting the existence of the organization, a serious breach — to protect his family's assets. He dies of lung cancer in custody in "Stage 5."

---

## Vincent Curatola

A businessman before acting, with no formal training. He got the audition through a chance encounter and had never played a substantial screen role.

He has spoken about the perp-walk scene as the hardest thing he was asked to do, and about the crew's genuine on-set discomfort watching it.
