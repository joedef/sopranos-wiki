## The seeker

Janice returns from Seattle in season two calling herself **Parvati**, having spent two decades cycling through communes, gurus, Buddhism, disability claims, and men with money. She has an answer for everything and has never once finished anything.

The joke — sustained across five seasons and never over-explained — is that all the spiritual vocabulary is a **delivery system for exactly her mother's behavior**. Janice is [Livia](livia-soprano.md) with better rhetoric. She manipulates, collects grievances, plays the victim, and weaponizes concern, and she does it while talking about healing.

She's also, unlike anyone else in the family, unafraid of Tony. Their fights are among the best-written material in the series because they're the fights of two people who know precisely where the other's soft tissue is and have known since childhood.

---

## Richie

She takes up with **[Richie Aprile](richie-aprile.md)**, recently out after ten years, and they're briefly well matched — two people whose sense of entitlement is total.

Then, at the dinner table, over an argument about his son, Richie punches her in the mouth.

She goes to the counter, comes back with a gun, and shoots him twice.

The scene is played almost casually. It's over in seconds. And then she calls Tony, who arrives, absorbs the situation, and has [Christopher](christopher-moltisanti.md) and [Furio](furio-giunta.md) dispose of the body while Janice sits and eats.

Tony's reaction is worth noting: not shock, not moral objection — **irritation**, at having to clean up after his sister again.

---

## Bobby

She then marries **[Bobby Baccalieri](bobby-baccalieri.md)**, the gentlest man in the organization, shortly after his wife's death — and the series is unambiguous that this is a project. She wants a man she can manage, and Bobby is the only available one.

Under her influence Bobby becomes more ambitious, more compromised, and eventually a killer. He's dead by "The Blue Comet."

The **anger management** material in season five — Janice attending classes, performing serenity, and then attacking a soccer mom in a parking lot — is the show's most efficient joke about her, and Turturro's best comic work.

## Aida Turturro

A stage actress and cousin of John Turturro. She had known **[James Gandolfini](../people/james-gandolfini.md)** since they appeared together in a 1992 Broadway production of *A Streetcar Named Desire*, which is a large part of why the sibling chemistry is so specific — the fights play like people with a shared history because they were.

Two Emmy nominations for the role.
