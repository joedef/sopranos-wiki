# The Ending

<p class="epigraph">Don't stop believin'.
</p>

At approximately 10:59pm Eastern on **June 10, 2007**, *The Sopranos* cut to black mid-scene and stayed there for about ten seconds before the credits rolled. A large portion of the audience thought their cable had failed.

This page is the factual account: what is on screen, what Chase has said, and what the major readings are. It deliberately does **not** tell you whether Tony dies. The interpretive argument is on [Reading the Ending](analysis/reading-the-ending.md); this page is the evidence.

---

## What is actually on screen

The episode is **"Made in America"** (S6E21), written and directed by David Chase, watched by **11.9 million** people — a 49% jump on the previous week.

The final scene is at **Holsten's Brookdale Confectionery** in Bloomfield, New Jersey, a real ice cream parlor that is still open.

The sequence, beat by beat:

1. **Tony arrives first** and slides into a booth, facing the door.
2. He flips through the tabletop **jukebox**. He selects **Journey's "Don't Stop Believin'"**, which begins to play. (A Little Feat track plays as he walks in, before the selection.)
3. A **man in a Members Only jacket** is already seated at the counter. He glances toward Tony more than once.
4. **Carmela** arrives. They talk. Tony orders **onion rings for the table**.
5. **A.J.** arrives and sits.
6. Two other men enter. A **Boy Scout troop** is in the room. The Members Only man keeps glancing over.
7. Outside, **Meadow** is repeatedly failing to parallel park. The film cuts to her attempts three times.
8. The **Members Only man gets up and walks to the restroom**, passing Tony's booth.
9. Meadow finally parks, crosses the street, and reaches the door.
10. The **door bell rings**. **Tony looks up.**
11. **Cut to black.** Silence. No music, no sound. Roughly ten seconds. Then credits.

Chase wanted **no credits at all** — just black — and did not get a Directors Guild waiver for it.

---

## The craft of it

Two technical points that most summaries miss, and that any reading has to account for:

**The editing pattern is a point-of-view structure.** Through the scene, the cutting establishes a rhythm: Tony looks up → cut to what he sees → cut back to Tony. It repeats each time the door opens. The final shot of Tony looking up arrives in that established rhythm — and the reverse shot never comes.

**The sound cuts too.** This is the part people forget. It isn't a fade or a dissolve into credits music. Picture and audio stop **at the same frame**, dead, mid-song, mid-word — Journey's "Don't stop—". Whatever else the ending is, it's constructed as an interruption rather than a conclusion.

## What Chase has actually said

His public statements have shifted over eighteen years. Presented chronologically, because the sequence is the interesting part:

| When | What he said |
| --- | --- |
| **June 2007** | *"I have no interest in explaining, defending, reinterpreting, or adding to what is there."* |
| **Oct 2007** | Rejected the idea that the ending was hostile to the audience — *"Why would we entertain people for eight years and then just tell them to go fuck themselves?"* — and said the ending said more than *"Tony facedown in a bowl of onion rings"* would have. Criticized viewers who wanted "justice." |
| **2015 (DGA Quarterly)** | *"The ceiling I was going for was 'don't stop believing.' It is very simple and on the nose."* |
| **Jan 2019 (*The Sopranos Sessions*)** | Referred in passing to *"that death scene."* Pressed on the phrase, he said **"Fuck you guys"** — then clarified he'd been describing an earlier, abandoned concept, not the filmed ending. |
| **Nov 2021 (*The Hollywood Reporter*)** | Discussing an early idea in which Tony died, he made a remark about a place "like that" — widely reported as confirming Tony dies at the diner. He then issued a statement: *"Everybody who believes I said Tony is dead in a Hollywood Reporter article: works for me."* |
| **Jan 2024 (TV Insider)** | Clarified that the scene implies Tony **could** die, not that he does. |
| **Sept 2024 (*Wise Guy* documentary)** | Went philosophical — *"the universe goes on and on. You may not go on and on, but the universe is going to go on and on"* — referenced the show's Robert Frost discussion, and the interview itself then cut to black. |

Read as a whole, that is not a man concealing an answer. It's a man who has spent eighteen years declining to convert an image into a fact, getting progressively more irritated at being asked, and occasionally enjoying himself.

## The load-bearing evidence people cite

**For "Tony dies":**

- The **Members Only jacket** — a callback to "Members Only" (S6E1), the episode in which Tony is shot, and slang for being a made man.
- **Bobby Baccalieri's line** in "Soprano Home Movies" (S6E13): that you probably don't hear it when it happens. Chase cuts back to that line in "The Blue Comet" (S6E20), which is unusual emphasis.
- **The Godfather parallel** — a man going to a restaurant bathroom and coming back out. Tony has named that scene as his favorite in his favorite film.
- **The POV structure** — the missing reverse shot means Tony's perception ends.
- The rhythm of Chase cutting to Tony's face on every door chime, then not.

**For "it doesn't resolve":**

- Chase has repeatedly framed the point as the *feeling*, not the fact — hypervigilance as the permanent condition of Tony's life. The audience is put in the seat of a man who has to look up at every door for the rest of his life.
- The three cuts to Meadow parking build a purely mundane suspense with no payoff by design.
- Carlo has flipped; the legal threat is arguably larger than the physical one, and it isn't resolved either.
- The **onion rings** placed like communion wafers, the family assembling — an ordinary evening that simply stops.

Actor **Matt Servitto** (Agent Harris) has said the scene as originally scripted continued, with the Members Only man returning from the restroom, and later clarified this didn't necessarily indicate a specific outcome.

## The reception, then and now

**Immediately:** mixed to hostile. Message boards melted. A widespread assumption of technical fault. Some viewers reported calling their cable providers.

**Within weeks:** reappraisal. Owen Gleiberman called it the perfect ending and praised how it made you replay the scene over and over. Maureen Ryan, initially angry, came around.

**Since:** near-consensus that it's among the great series finales. TV Guide has placed it at or near the top of its finales lists; the standing observation is that the fact people are still arguing is the proof it worked.

It won **the 2007 Primetime Emmy for Outstanding Writing for a Drama Series** — Chase's third writing Emmy for the show. Editor **Sidney Wolinsky** won the 2008 Eddie Award for it.
